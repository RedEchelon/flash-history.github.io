Methodology

Humor as a learning tool has had a long, long history. There are citations of its usage going back to ancient Greece. "Euclid, a famous ancient Greek mathematician who is known as the father of geometry, once was teaching the first theorem of geometry in class. He found a student lost focus and kept on whispering to desk mate and doing petty actions. Euclid paused his lecture and reminded the distracted student to swift back his attention to class. Unexpectedly, the student suddenly stood up and asked: “What on earth was the concrete benefits of learning geometry.” Euclid did not answer the question after a moment of silence, instead, he ordered his servant and said: “Please get some money and give it to this gentleman, he would never study without ‘concrete benefit’.” The student was overwhelmed with shame. Euclid resolved the surprised situation in class and corrected the wrong thoughts of the student by a humorous way instead of criticizing him directly." (Zhou 2025). Its usage in the context of learning is often referred to as instructional or teaching humor, has been studied for nearly as long as it's been used. Studies have found that, when humor was involved in learning, students were more likely to retain information over a long period of time. (Kaplan 1977). The preponderance of evidence suggests that, while learning can of course happen even if material is quite dry, humor does often assist to a degree. This heavily informed the casual tone I elected to take with this work. If my goal is to get people interested in the history of Flash, I should make them laugh, or at least smile. I should include easter eggs and personal details. People remember things like that, more than they will remember the facts of who FutureSplash is. Those easter eggs are the essence of what this era was, to me. I don't just want my audience to dryly learn information they can regurgitate at a party in the future; I want to stir some kind of pathos. Entertainment is an easy way to get someone to feel something, so I went for that as my focus. The goal of this project was to encourage the reader to remember their time with art made in Flash during the 2000s, and possibly engender an interest in working with the archivists at Flashpoint. The subject matter itself is often informal, and I felt it prudent to match that tone. If I could get my viewers to feel a connection with the material by telling jokes, or mentioning that I think the crab is cute, or complaining about George Liquor, that could create the kind of strength of relationship that Zhou describes: "The clearest and most consistent empirical finding regarding instructional humor in educational settings is its affective effects on students. Teachers can employ humor to foster a welcoming classroom environment and strengthen their relationships with students. This positive affective experience can, in turn, boost student engagement and participation." (Zhou 2025). If I want my viewers to engage with the material and, possibly, participate in its preservation, I needed the project to be funny and charming and a little bit off-kilter, and so I focused hard on that tone as I worked.
