Thoughts on the project

I've spent a really long time thinking about Flash. Flash informed so much of my childhood. Every website I went to was made in it, and the internet was a home to me. An escape. It's only natural that you'd become nostalgic for something like that.

It's informed the woman I am now, and the archivist I am now. I realized I wanted to go into library science because I got to thinking about how underdiscussed this kind of art is when it comes to the archives. There's not really a lot of professional support for the archival of Flash - largely, it's just a bunch of volunteers, doing it out of love for the art. And that's great! Volunteers can do a lot that professional institutions can't! It just...without professional support, a single company changing their mind about turning a blind eye, and everything falls apart.

So this became my passion, and something I would write about as much as I could. If there wasn't professional support, I would do what I could to create that support. If there weren't enough volunteers, I would use the skills I had to create writing that would inspire people to care like I do, like Raven Carr, co-lead of the Flashpoint Archive, and all my other friends who work on Flashpoint do.

That drive inspired this project. A drive to do everything I can for a time period I grew up loving, and art that meant everything to me.

...Well, that makes for a simple story, but it's not entirely true. I made a storymap version of this first. That project was something I was really proud of. I liked the writing I did, and the ways it showed off the material and got across the connections between websites, but...

Well, for one thing, I don't think the connections thing came across that well? It came across less like the way that websites would interconnect during the internet of my childhood, and more like...

Not really the vibe I was trying to go for. I also think it was a little all-over-the-place. A lot of short blurbs about a million different topics, without really a strong focus. A future version would be best to compress things down into a smaller number of denser slides.

And then, on top of that, a thought struck me. If I wanted to drive interest in Flash, and I was talking up how it was still alive...

Why in the world was I doing this thing in storymap.js?

If I want to prove that Flash is still alive and worthwhile, why not put my money where my mouth is, and make the thing in Flash?

My girlfriend would describe this as a "coyote idea". Like, the kind of thing that Wile E. Coyote would come up with, shortly before blowing himself up, or falling off a cliff, or being squashed with an anvil. But I just couldn't get this really work-intensive idea that would probably create a worse product overall out of my head.

Everytime I saw a Flash game as a kid, or an animation, or anything like it, I wanted to make something similar. I remember first having the thought to do it at 9 years old, and that was 15 years ago at this point. Sure, this was a bad idea, probably. Yeah, it would limit what I can actually do. But when am I ever gonna get another opportunity to learn and use Flash for something productive?

So a couple months ago I contacted my friend Raven Carr, co-lead of the Flashpoint Archive, to see if she could help me scrounge up a copy of Adobe Flash CS6. She happened to have one, by sheer luck, so I tried to get it set up.

So, obviously it's not really feasible to purchase and activate Flash anymore after it's end-of-life. The copy I borrowed from Raven required quite a lot of setup, and that took me about a solid week. I had to fully disconnect my computer from the internet while it was setting up, and this is before I even had the chance to learn anything about the program. Eventually, I had to call Raven on the phone to have her help me get it working, and even that took more than an hour. Still, she helped me get it set up. If you're reading this, Raven, thank you so much for that.

And then after the setup I had to actually learn the program. Which was...a process. I have never been much of a programming expert, shall we say. And Actionscript 3 is what we would call a...programming language hardly anyone uses for anything in the last decade. My initial plan was to take apart a BBC-made Flash game about the history of ship navigation, see what made that thing tick, and then try and imitate it in my own project. Reverse engineering. This didn't really work out. That thing was exceptionally complicated for what I needed to do. It also just...didn't have enough text-per-slide for the density I'd been envisioning, and I didn't have the graphic design skills to make anything that looked nearly this good with vectors, frankly.

Eventually, I hit pay-dirt with some old tutorials for making slideshows in Flash, which suited my needs fine, and didn't require me to sift through hundreds of lines of a programming language I did not remotely understand. Thank god. I had enough trouble with ActionScript in the thing I did make. Once I had this figured out, I moved on to getting templates going for me to slot text and images into. Initially, I planned to just do Windows 95, 98, XP, and 7. I thought "Well, what's the point of bothering with Windows 3.0 for literally one slide total, when nobody would even think about the fact that 1994 shouldn't be covered with 95."

Then I had another thought. What's the point of bothering with any of this in Flash instead of doing it in a more sensible format? Why is Windows 3.0, a visual style that is very easy to imitate, the line in the sand I'm drawing, instead of any of the way harder stuff I'm doing for the rest of this? So, I ended up doing Windows 3.0, too. Once I made 3.0 and 95, I started writing up some slides. There's a strong argument SmartSketch and FutureSplash could have just been one slide, but I think it's valuable to mention both. Besides, I had to show the funny crab. Talking about Flash history has given me a lot of affection for that guy. Try clicking on him in the Flash thing on the main page!

I wanted to embed some FLV videos of George Liquor, Fishbar, and especially Homestar Runner to give the viewer an instant idea of the specific types of media you'd see in internet Flash animations of the time, but it turns out that Ruffle's emulation doesn't support it, so I had to drop that idea. It's a shame, because I think it would have made the whole thing feel more immersive. Still, I did throw an easter egg onto the slide about early Flash animation. Try closing the window if you want to hear me complain about how terrible George Liquor is.

Writing was largely uneventful for most of the project. I found Windows XP and 7 UI kits on Figma, which I used to create the templates for the slides of that era. Everything was going pretty smoothly. I added the NewGrounds pre-loader from their website, started adding links to videos and little animations, it was all going great.

And then I got to the slide about Thoughts On Flash, and everything started going wrong. It felt like I was driving my car along the road, and suddenly two wheels fell off, the engine caught fire, lightning struck the antenna, a gang of roving bandits started stealing my hubcaps, the air conditioner stopped working, and the check engine light came on all at once. Flash started taking several minutes to open my project - a project that is only about 8 MB in size! I tried to edit the text, and there was a several second delay before any text appeared onscreen, and it would only appear once I stopped typing. Attempting to rename the text box to associate the scroll bar with it caused the program to crash. Changing the font of the text caused the program to crash. Copying and pasting ANYTHING caused the program to crash. I just needed the duct tape to hold the car together long enough to get me to the finish line, but man was that duct tape ever fraying. Eventually, after much fighting, I got the project into a state I was happy with.

So...my opinion on Flash as a piece of software has shifted a bit. For years, I felt that Flash was unfairly maligned, and that Thoughts On Flash was a largely unfair piece of writing by a guy who does, to be fair, tend to be understood to have been quite awful as a person. I don't...think that, anymore.

Well, I still think parts of it are unfair, and obviously I still think Steve Jobs was awful.

That said...Flash had to die, so the internet could live. The program was sputtering and smoking and coughing up blood in a project that was, essentially, a slideshow. With some images and some text-boxes. It didn't even have the embedded videos anymore, and Flash just did not want to make it happen. This software was the equivalent to a janky jalopy car that is just begging for you to take it to the junkyard.

I felt like I was someone's uncle working on a project muscle car in my driveway. I still love the art made in Flash, but I'm not particularly enamored with the software anymore. The early internet was so creative not because of this janky jalopy software that hates you for trying to program anything more complicated than a frame-by-frame animation, but because of the people. That's why I highlighted Homestar Runner in all versions of this project, and why I'm linking to it in the sidebar. That was a years-long passion project made by two brothers and some friends and family because they loved to do it! That's the spirit of what drew me to all this! The Brothers Chaps would have found some way to make Homestar Runner, Flash or no Flash. Tom Fulp would have made something like Pico's School, Flash or no Flash.

As much nostalgia as I have for the era, I don't care about the software nearly as much anymore. The history was interesting, but I've become much more interested in the personal histories of the creatives. If I ever make a follow-up to this project, that will be the core of it. People who made art for fun, or for profit, or for whatever motive, and just put it out there. This whole world of art that you can find online!

Certainly, I will not be making a follow-up in Flash. I have had my fill. I swear I've noticed a few gray hairs happen after I made that Thoughts On Flash slide. Thank God Flash is dead, man. Imagine having to deal with the internet still running on this piece of software that sputters and flames out at the slightest touch.
